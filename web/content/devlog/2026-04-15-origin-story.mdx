## The Itch

It started late at night, around 2 AM, with a city builder I'd been playing for months.

I'd just finished meticulously laying out a transit corridor — bus lanes, tram stops, pedestrian bridges, the whole thing. It looked great on the map. Then I watched as my citizens, blessed with perfect transit access, promptly drove their individual cars across town to the one job center I'd accidentally placed on the wrong side of a highway.

The game didn't care. The simulation had decided, somewhere beneath the surface, that driving was always better. My transit investment was theater.

I closed the game and opened a blank document.

## What I Actually Want

I've been a game designer, a musician, a physics student, and a software engineer — in roughly that order, with a lot of overlap. The thing that's always pulled me back to city builders is the same thing that pulls me to music: **emergent complexity from simple rules**.

A jazz standard is twelve bars. Everyone knows the changes. And yet every performance is different, because the rules interact with the moment, the room, the players.

I want Territory to feel like that.

Not a city builder where you place buildings and watch numbers tick up. A city builder where the **terrain shapes what's possible**, where **water and roads and height all push back**, where a good decision today creates interesting constraints tomorrow.

I want to feel the city's logic, not just see its statistics.

## The Design Commitments

A few things I've decided Territory will be:

**Isometric, tile-based, with real elevation.** Slope matters. Building on a hillside is genuinely different from building on a plain. Water flows downhill. Roads cost more to cut through terrain.

**No omniscient zoning.** You don't draw residential zones and then watch houses appear. You lay infrastructure, create conditions, and then see what emerges. Citizens have actual constraints — they respond to access, cost, and proximity in ways you can observe and influence, but not fully control.

**Transparent simulation.** When something goes wrong, you should be able to trace why. The game should reward attention. Mystery is fine; opacity is not.

**Small, legible scope.** I'm one person. I'm building this after work and on weekends. Territory will be small, tight, and finished — not a grand megaproject that quietly dies at 60% completion.

## Where It Is Now

I've been building Territory in Unity for several months. The terrain system works — height maps, water, shore bands, cliff faces. Roads can be placed and they respond to terrain cost. The grid is real. The height is real. Water actually flows.

What doesn't exist yet: the economy, the demand system, the actual city-building loop. That's what the next phase of work is about.

This devlog is where I'll document what I'm building, why I'm making the decisions I'm making, and what I'm learning along the way.

I don't know if Territory will ever be a "real" game. But I know it's already more interesting to build than to play most of the games that frustrated me into starting it.

That feels like a good sign.
